The Ethics of Anonymity and Surveys

Marcus Julius Estrella | Jun 10, 2026 min read

Surveys and Ethics

In this blog, we shall explore the idea around a specific survey and how ethics is related to it.

We shall explore issues around the said survey and how it will affect both the surveyor and the participants.

The Problem

During this week, you learned how to decide what is right and wrong when conducting data science through concepts, techniques, and cautionary tales. In this assignment, you will create a blog post that analyzes a problem from a data science ethics perspective, applying the concepts you learned this week.

At the completion of a course, participants are given a survey of 10 questions. To perform the survey, participants access a URL in their web browser that is made available for a short period of time and requires that students log in with their username and password for the organization.

The beginning of the survey prompts for the name of the course, but not their name. Instead, the survey description on this page indicates that all answers and the survey itself will remain anonymous.

Executive management wishes to use the results of the survey to determine which courses require redesign or updating. You are the data analyst assigned to this task, and are required to provide actionable insights for this purpose to executive management.

Survey

Questions

Consider the Following Questions:

  1. Based on the information provided, what ethical issues are apparent in the design of this survey, and in the sample question?
  2. What other ethical issues could be present for this survey?
  3. What recommendations would you make to executive management to address 1) and 2)?
  4. If executive management rejects your recommendations and insists that the survey and system have no issues, ethical or otherwise, what are a few ways you could proceed ethically?

Issues in the Design and Question

Questionable Anonymity

The survey states that responses are anonymous, but participants must first log in using their student username and password. Even if login is only used for authentication and responses are later separated from identities, participants may reasonably believe their responses can be linked back to them. If login information is retained alongside survey responses, then the survey is not anonymous, it is merely confidential.

Lack of Transparency

The survey description says responses will remain anonymous, but participants are not told:

  1. why login is required,
  2. whether login data is stored
  3. who has access to the data
  4. how long data is retained
  5. and, how anonymity is protected

Response Bias

There is a tendency of participants to answer survey or interview questions inaccurately or untruthfully. Potential issues that may arise includes:

  • It assumes participants understand every course objective.
  • It measures only one aspect of course quality.
  • It encourages a single overall judgment rather than identifying which objectives were or were not met.

This limits the usefulness of the results.

Scale Design

The scale runs from 0 to 10 with “Strongly disagree” and “Strongly agree”. Possible issues include:

  • The meaning of intermediate values (e.g., 4, 5, 6) is unclear
  • A midpoint is present but not labeled as “Neutral”
  • Different participants may interpret the numbers differently, reducing reliability

Limited Actionability

Executive management wants actionable insights for course redesign. However, a single agreement statement provides little diagnostic information. A low score does not explain: - which objectives failed - whether the instructor, materials, assessments, technology, or pacing caused the problem

Other Issues That Could Be Present

Selection Bias

Only participants who choose to complete the survey are represented. Those with particularly positive or negative experiences may be more likely to respond.

Nonresponse Bias

If participation is voluntary, the results may not accurately represent all learners.

Fear of Retaliation

Fear of retaliation - Even if responses are anonymous, employees may worry that criticism could affect:

  • performance evaluations
  • promotions
  • future training opportunities This fear can produce artificially positive responses.

Timing Effects

If the survey is only available for a short time:

  • some learners may miss it
  • responses may come primarily from people immediately after completing the course, rather than after applying the knowledge

Accessibility

Ethically, surveys should be usable by participants with disabilities, including compatibility with screen readers, keyboard navigation, and appropriate visual design.

Data Security

There is a need to know if the data being gathered by the survey is secure. This includes your personal information and answers.

  • Is the data encrypted?
  • Who can access it?
  • How long is it retained?
  • Is it protected against unauthorized access?

Poor security creates ethical and legal risks.

Privacy Compliance

Depending on the jurisdiction, the survey may be subject to privacy laws or organizational policies regarding employee data collection.

Misuse of Results

There is a chance that the information received will be misused by the executive managers to compare instructors unfairly. They could also evaluate employee performance using anonymous survey data. And lastly, they could make decisions without considering response rates or statistical significance.

Recommendations for Executive Management

Here are several improvements that would strengthen both ethics and data quality in the survey.

Improve transparency

In order to improve the transparency, here are the things that are necessary to explain

  • why login is required
  • whether responses can be linked to identities
  • who can access the data
  • how long data will be stored
  • how anonymity or confidentiality is protected

Ensure True Anonymity

If anonymity is promised, here is a list of what to do:

  • We should authenticate users before the survey.
  • There is a need to immediately separate authentication records from survey responses.
  • We must avoid storing identifying information with responses.

Improve Survey Design

The broad questions of the survey should be replaced with more specific items such as learning objectives, course materials, instructor effectiveness, pacing, assessments, and practical usefulness.

It is also a good idea to include open-ended questions and a space to answer these questions. These questions include:

  • What worked well?
  • What should be improved?
  • What topics need additional coverage?

Improve Measurement Quality

In order to improve the accuracy of the survey, we need to label all the points clearly and not just the first and last numbers as shown on the survey. This will also include a “Neutral” option if appropriate.

Reduce Bias

  • Participation should be voluntary in order for the ones who truly care for the survey to answer.
  • It should also be clear that responses in the survey would not affect the professor or teacher in question.
  • Allow sufficient time for students to finish the survey.
  • Monitor response rates before drawing a conclusion.

Alternative Measures

Document the Concerns

We should document the concerns and recommendations in writing so there is a clear record that potential ethical and methodological issues were identified.

Communication is Key

When presenting the survey results, the analysis should accurately describe the limitations of the data. Any concerns regarding possible response bias, uncertainty about anonymity, or weaknesses in the survey design should be clearly communicated. The findings should not be presented as more reliable or conclusive than the evidence supports.

Follow Professional Code of Ethics

It is also important to follow relevant professional standards by protecting participant privacy, maintaining honesty in reporting, and avoiding misleading conclusions. If asked to state that the survey is anonymous when this cannot be verified, or to make unsupported claims about the accuracy of the findings, those statements should be respectfully declined.

Escalate to Higher-Ups if Necessary

If there is evidence that the survey violates organizational policies, legal requirements, or accepted ethical standards, the concerns should be raised through appropriate internal channels such as a supervisor, ethics office, privacy officer, or internal audit function. Throughout the process, the data analyst should remain objective, transparent, and committed to producing accurate analyses that support responsible decision-making.

Conclusion

The primary ethical concern is the apparent contradiction between requiring authenticated login and promising anonymity. This could undermine participant trust and the validity of the data. Beyond privacy, issues such as response bias, unclear survey design, insufficient transparency, and limited actionability reduce both the ethical integrity and practical value of the survey. By improving transparency, protecting respondent privacy, redesigning questions to generate actionable insights, and communicating analytical limitations honestly, the organization can produce more reliable data while respecting participants’ rights.
